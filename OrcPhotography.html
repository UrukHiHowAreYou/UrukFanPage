Capturing the shy orc in his native habitat:
A beginner's guide

Saruman the Wise
February

"There is no 'symbolism' or conscious allegory in my story," insisted J. R. R. Tolkien. "To ask if the Orcs 'are' Communists is to me as sensible as asking if Communists are Orcs" (Tolkien 2006: 262). In spite of this forthright dismissal, readings along these lines have long been a consistent feature of Tolkien's reception history: with Kirill Eskov's splendid counter-factual fantasy The Last Ringbearer (1999) being the most entertaining and well-known of such readings. The first Swedish edition of The Lord of the Rings, for example, explains to the reader that Sauron, the personification of the satanic, is to be read perhaps as Stalin. "There is no 'perhaps' about it," raged Tolkien. "I utterly repudiate any such 'reading,' which angers me."

Explaining his story was conceived long before the Russian revolution, Tolkien insists that "Such allegory is entirely foreign to my thought" (Tolkien 2006: 307). And in a foreword to the second English-language edition, Tolkien felt compelled to point out that the War of the Ring resembles the Second World War neither in its process, nor in its conclusion.
"I think that many confuse 'applicability' with 'allegory,'" he suggests. "An author cannot of course remain unaffected by his experience, but the ways in which a story-germ uses the soil of experience are extremely complex, and attempts to define the process are at best guesses from evidence that is inadequate and ambiguous."
In order to be quite clear, Tolkien explains that "I cordially dislike allegory in all its manifestations [and] I much prefer history, true or feigned, with its varied applicability to the thought and experience of readers" (Tolkien 1966: xvi-xvii).
